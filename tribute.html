The Life and Legacy of Nelson Mandela: 1918-2013

  • 1918 - Rolihlahla Dalibhunga Mandela is born a member of the Madiba clan. His tribal name, "Rolihalah," means "troublemaker." He is later given his English name, Nelson, by a teacher at his school
  • 1938 - Mandela is admitted to the University of Fort Hare, a black institution. Two years later he was expelled for leading a student protest.
  • 1944 - Mandela co-founded the ANC Youth League and marries Evelyn Ntoko Mase. The couple had four children, but his political activities put a strain on their relationship and they were divorced in 1958.
  • 1948 - He was elected National Secretary of the ANC Youth League. Also the National Party, dominated by white Dutch-descended Afrikaners, is elected to power and begins installing apartheid, a system of complete racial segregation. It will rule without interruption for 46 years.
  • 1952 - He is Volunteer-in-Chief of the Defiance Campaign; is sentenced to nine months in prison suspended for two years with 19 others for their role in the campaign; is banned for the first time; is asked to leave Wits over unpaid debt; starts South Africa’s first black law firm, Mandela & Tambo
  • 1961 - Is acquitted in Treason Trial; goes underground; calls on Verwoerd to set up talks on a non-racial future for South Africa; calls for a three-day strike in May over South Africa becoming a republic; is selected as Commander-in-Chief of Umkhonto we Sizwe
  • 1962 - Secretly leaves the country for military training and to get support for the armed struggle; arrested on 5 August; sentenced to five years in prison for leaving the country without a passport and encouraging the strike
  • 1964 - He and seven others are convicted and sentenced to life in prison. He is sent to Robben Island prison, seven miles off the coast of Cape Town and will spend the next 18 years there.
  • 1976 - Tens of thousands of students take to the streets of Soweto to oppose the use of Afrikaans as the language of instruction in black schools. The police fire on the protesters, setting off months of violence that will leave more than 570 people dead. The uprising is considered a turning point in the history of black resistance to apartheid
  • 1989 - Meets President P.W. Botha at the presidential office in Cape Town. It is the first publicly acknowledged meeting between Mr. Mandela and a government official outside prison. Meets Botha's successor, F.W. de Klerk
  • 1990 - Nelson Mandela is released from prison after 27 years, President De Klerk lifts the ban on the African National Congress (ANC), The ANC and the white National Party begin talks on forming a multi-racial democracy for South Africa.
  • 1993 - Divorces Winnie Mandela; is jointly awarded the Nobel Peace Prize with FW de Klerk for their efforts to end apartheid
  • 1994 - Mandela inaugurated as South Africa's first black president after ANC wins South Africa's first all-race election; his autobiography, Long Walk to Freedom, is published
  • 1999 - Mandela steps down after one term, a rarity among African presidents, but continues to be active in causes promoting world peace, supporting children and fighting AIDS.
  • 2004 - Finally retires from public life; flew to Bangkok to speak at the XV International AIDS Conference. Johannesburg bestowed its highest honour by granting Nelson Mandela the freedom of the city
  • 2012 - Mandela spends nearly three weeks in a hospital, where he is treated for a lung infection and has a procedure to remove gallstones.
  • 2013 - The government says Mandela is admitted to a hospital with a recurring lung infection. Officials describe his condition as serious but stable; died in December 5 at age 95.

I have fought against white domination, and I have fought against black domination. I have cherished the ideal of a democratic and free society in which all persons will live together in harmony and with equal opportunities. It is an ideal which I hope to live for and to see realised. But if it needs be, it is an ideal for which I am prepared to die.

Mandela's Rivonia Trial Speech, 1964
